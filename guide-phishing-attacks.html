Section 01

What Is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyberattack where criminals impersonate trusted entities — banks, tech companies, government agencies, or even your colleagues — to trick you into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

The term "phishing" comes from the idea of fishing for victims using a baited hook. Attackers cast their net wide, sending millions of deceptive emails hoping that even a small fraction of recipients will take the bait.

3.4B Phishing emails sent every single day
90% Of data breaches start with phishing
$17,700 Lost per minute to phishing attacks globally
🚨
Why This Matters to You

You don't need to be a celebrity or corporation to be targeted. Most phishing attacks are automated and target anyone with an email address. One successful attack can result in identity theft, financial loss, or account takeovers.

🧠
Section 02

Why Phishing Works

Phishing is so effective not because it exploits software vulnerabilities — it exploits human psychology. Attackers are experts at manufacturing urgency, fear, and trust.

⚡
Urgency & Fear

"Your account will be suspended in 24 hours!" — panic short-circuits critical thinking. You act before you think.

🏢
Authority Impersonation

Attackers clone logos, email styles, and language from brands you trust — Google, PayPal, your bank, your boss.

🎁
Tempting Rewards

"You've won a $500 gift card!" — greed is as powerful as fear. Offers seem too good to pass up.

🔏
False Security

Fake padlock icons, "secure" wording, and HTTPS links create a false sense of legitimacy and safety.

💡
The Golden Rule

Legitimate organizations will never ask for your password, full credit card number, or one-time codes via email. If an email requests these, it is a scam — no exceptions.

Section 03

Types of Phishing Attacks

Phishing has evolved far beyond generic spam emails. Modern attacks are targeted, personalized, and delivered through multiple channels.

📧

Email Phishing

HIGH

The classic attack — mass emails impersonating trusted brands sent to millions of addresses. Even if only 0.1% respond, it's profitable.

Example: "Your PayPal account has been limited. Click here to restore access." — sent from paypa1-support@secure-login.net
🎯

Spear Phishing

CRITICAL

Highly targeted attacks on specific individuals. Attackers research their victim on LinkedIn, social media, and company websites to craft personalized, convincing messages.

Example: "Hi Sarah, as discussed in yesterday's meeting, please review and sign the attached NDA." — sent impersonating your actual CEO using their real name and signature.
🐳

Whaling

CRITICAL

Spear phishing targeting senior executives (CEOs, CFOs) — "big fish." One successful whaling attack can transfer millions in company funds.

Example: Fake email to a CFO from "legal counsel" instructing an urgent wire transfer for a confidential acquisition deal.
📱

Smishing & Vishing

HIGH

SMS-based phishing (smishing) and voice call phishing (vishing). People trust texts and phone calls more than emails, making these extremely effective.

Example: "ALERT: Your bank account has been compromised. Call 1-800-XXX-XXXX immediately." — the number connects to a scammer posing as bank security.
🔍

Search Engine Phishing

MEDIUM

Fake websites that rank in search results for common queries like "PayPal login" or "bank online banking". Users click what looks like an official result and land on a credential-stealing clone.

Example: Searching "Netflix login" and clicking an ad that leads to netflix-account-secure.com — a perfect visual clone of the real site.
🔬
Section 04

Anatomy of a Phishing Attack

Understanding the step-by-step playbook attackers use helps you anticipate and recognize attacks before you're compromised.

1

Reconnaissance

The attacker researches their target — browsing LinkedIn for job titles, checking company websites for email formats, and mining social media for personal details to make the attack convincing.

2

Infrastructure Setup

They register lookalike domains (paypa1.com, googIe.com), clone the target brand's website pixel-for-pixel, and set up fake email addresses to pass basic spam filters.

3

Crafting the Lure

The email is designed to trigger an emotional response — urgency ("your account expires in 2 hours"), fear ("suspicious login detected"), or greed ("you have a pending refund").

4

Delivery

Emails are sent in bulk or targeted delivery, often timed for Monday mornings or Friday afternoons when people are distracted. Attachments may contain malware; links redirect to fake login pages.

5

Credential Harvesting

When you enter credentials on the fake page, they're captured instantly. Some attackers show a "login failed" message to prompt a second attempt, then redirect to the real site — you never suspect anything went wrong.

6

Exploitation

Stolen credentials are immediately used to access accounts, transferred, or sold on the dark web. Attackers may also persist silently in inboxes — forwarding emails, studying financial conversations, then striking at the perfect moment.

Section 05

Red Flags to Spot Instantly

Train your eye to spot these warning signs. No single flag is definitive, but multiple flags together almost always indicate a phishing attempt.

🔤
Mismatched or lookalike sender domain — The display name says "PayPal Support" but the actual email is from paypa1-secure@login-verify.net. Always expand and check the full email address.
⚡
Extreme urgency or threats — "Act now or your account will be permanently deleted in 24 hours!" Real companies don't threaten you with immediate account deletion via cold email.
🔗
Suspicious links that don't match — Hover over links (don't click) to see the real destination URL. If the visible text says "paypal.com" but the URL shows "paypa1-secure.xyz/login", it's fake.
📎
Unexpected attachments — Unsolicited attachments — especially .exe, .zip, .docm files or PDFs you didn't request — are a major red flag. They may contain malware that installs silently.
👋
Generic greetings — "Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name. Legitimate services you have accounts with know your name and use it.
✍️
Grammar and spelling errors — Phishing emails often contain awkward phrasing, odd punctuation, or spelling mistakes. Professional companies proofread their communications.
🔑
Requests for sensitive information — Any email asking for your password, full credit card number, Social Security number, or bank details is a scam. No exceptions.
🎁
Too-good-to-be-true offers — "You've been selected for a free iPhone!" or "Claim your $1,000 reward now!" If you didn't enter a contest, you didn't win anything.
🔍

Dissecting a Real Phishing Email

Let's analyze a typical phishing email. Hover over the annotated flags to understand exactly what makes this dangerous.

📧 Mail — Inbox
From: PayPal Support <support@paypa1-secure-login.com> ⚠ LOOKALIKE DOMAIN
To: you@youremail.com
Subject: ⚠ ACTION REQUIRED: Your PayPal account has been limited ⚠ URGENCY

Dear Customer, ⚠ GENERIC GREETING

We have noticed suspicious activity on your PayPal account. To protect your funds, we have temporarily limited your account access.

You must verify your identity within 24 hours or your account will be permanently suspended and any pending payments will be cancelled. ⚠ FALSE URGENCY + THREAT

Click the button below to verify your account:

Verify My Account Now →
⚠ LINK GOES TO FAKE SITE

Link destination: http://paypa1-secure-login.com/verify?ref=83921 ⚠ NOT PAYPAL.COM

If you did not initiate this request, please ignore this email.
PayPal Security Team

🛑
This email contains 5 major red flags

Lookalike domain · Generic greeting · Artificial urgency · Threat of account loss · Link to non-PayPal domain. Any one of these is suspicious. All five together = definite phishing. Never click that link.

Section 07

How to Protect Yourself

Defense against phishing is multi-layered. No single measure is enough — combine these practices for strong protection.

✓

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) everywhere

Even if attackers steal your password, MFA means they still can't access your account without your phone or authenticator app. Use an authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy) rather than SMS codes where possible.

✓

Always verify links before clicking

Hover over any link to see the actual destination URL in your browser's status bar. If the real URL doesn't match the displayed text or isn't the brand's official domain, don't click. When in doubt, navigate directly by typing the URL yourself.

✓

Use a password manager

Password managers auto-fill credentials only on legitimate websites. If you land on a phishing clone, the manager won't fill in your password — a built-in protection against credential theft.

✓

Verify unexpected requests through a separate channel

If your "CEO" emails you asking for an urgent wire transfer, call them directly using a number you already have — not one provided in the email. This single habit stops most spear phishing and whaling attacks.

✓

Keep software and browsers updated

Phishing sometimes leads to malware downloads. Updated browsers and OS block many known malicious sites and patch exploits that malicious attachments rely on.

✓

Use an AI-powered email scanner

Tools like PhishScan AI analyze emails for lookalike domains, urgency patterns, suspicious links, and header anomalies — catching attacks that bypass traditional spam filters.

✓

Report phishing emails — don't just delete them

Report phishing to your email provider (Gmail: "Report phishing", Outlook: "Report message") and forward to report@phishing.gov or the impersonated company's abuse team. This helps protect others.
